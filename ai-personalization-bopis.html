AI Personalization & BOPIS Rollout

How behavioral data, ML personalization, and omnichannel fulfillment came together to deliver a 63% net sales improvement and 7% churn reduction for an enterprise retail brand.

Key Problem

Revenue lost at discovery — and again at fulfillment

Enterprise retail brands were losing revenue to two compounding failures: poor product discovery online, and underutilized store inventory with no digital connection. Customers browsing large catalogs could not find what they wanted — not because the product did not exist, but because there was no personalization layer to surface the right item to the right person at the right moment.

At the same time, physical stores were sitting on inventory the digital channel had zero visibility into. There was no mechanism for a customer to choose same-day in-store pickup, and no operational system to route that intent to the nearest location. The result: missed conversions online, idle inventory in stores, and churn at both ends of the funnel.

Two distinct product problems — discovery and fulfillment — were compounding into one revenue and retention problem. Solving only one would underdeliver. The opportunity required both tracks running in parallel.

Core Users

Two users. One system to serve both.

Online Shoppers

Browsing large retail catalogs with high intent but low confidence in finding the right product. Needed relevant recommendations across PDP, PLP, and cart — not generic bestsellers. Also needed fulfillment flexibility: order online, pick up same day without waiting on shipping.

In-Store Associates

Receiving and fulfilling BOPIS orders alongside regular floor duties. Needed clear pick-list workflows, real-time inventory visibility, and a notification system that did not create operational chaos at peak hours or during high-volume events.

Action

Two tracks. Tight feedback loops. Parallel execution.

Track 1 · AI Personalization Engine

Partnered with BI and data science teams to design behavioral analytics pipelines connecting customer signals — browse history, add-to-cart behavior, session patterns, purchase recency — to product recommendation surfaces across PDP, PLP, and cart. Defined conversion-tied KPIs before a single model was trained, ensuring measurement design preceded technical execution rather than being retrofitted afterward.

Ran controlled A/B experiments to validate personalization impact in isolation before expanding placement. Each experiment was designed with a clear hypothesis, holdout group, and minimum detectable effect — avoiding the common failure mode of launching personalization broadly before understanding which behavioral signals actually drove conversion lift.

Worked with engineering to integrate the recommendation engine into page surfaces with minimal latency impact. Weekly experiment readouts fed directly into the next sprint's prioritization — keeping the feedback loop between behavioral data and roadmap tight and continuous.

Track 2 · BOPIS End-to-End

Owned BOPIS from customer discovery through post-launch measurement. Ran structured discovery with store operations leads and customers to map the full journey — surfacing the key failure points: inventory lookup latency at checkout, ambiguous pickup confirmation UX, and associate workflow gaps at the store level that would undermine the experience even if the digital layer worked perfectly.

Led sprint execution for the digital layer — checkout BOPIS option, real-time inventory availability, and confirmation plus ready-to-pickup notification flows — while coordinating with store operations on associate tooling, pick-list design, and go-live readiness across locations.

Sequenced the rollout deliberately — piloting in a subset of stores first to stress-test the operational workflow before full launch. Post-launch measurement tracked net sales, inventory utilization, and BOPIS NPS independently from general checkout performance, so impact attribution was clean.

Key architectural decision:

Both tracks had shared infrastructure dependencies — inventory APIs, customer identity, and checkout orchestration. Rather than sequencing the work, we surfaced those shared dependencies early and defined a common contract layer, allowing personalization and BOPIS to develop in parallel without blocking each other. That decision alone saved 6+ weeks of sequential delivery time.

Impact

Outcomes that moved the business

BOPIS Results

63% improvement in net sales attributed to BOPIS, driven by converting browse-and-abandon into same-day fulfillment. 50% increase in local store inventory utilization — stores that were previously a cost center became an active fulfillment asset.

Personalization Results

15% increase in digital revenue validated through behavioral analytics and iterative A/B testing. 7% reduction in customer churn YoY — better discovery and fulfillment flexibility closed the primary drop-off points that had been driving abandonment.

Learning

What this reinforced

Omnichannel success requires store operations alignment as much as product execution. The go-live coordination across store ops, marketing, and engineering was as critical as the build itself — the digital product worked, but a poorly coordinated rollout would have buried the signal in operational noise during the first weeks of launch.

Personalization only delivers value when the feedback loop between behavioral data and roadmap is tight and continuous. Launching a recommendation engine and moving on is not a product — it is a feature. The value compounds through iteration, not installation.

And running two parallel tracks reinforced something fundamental: shared infrastructure dependencies need to be surfaced and resolved early, not discovered mid-sprint. Map the shared contracts first. Build in parallel after.
